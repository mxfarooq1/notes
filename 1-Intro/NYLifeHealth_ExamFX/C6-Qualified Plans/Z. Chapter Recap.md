In this chapter you learned about different types of individual and group qualified plans and their characteristics. Let’s recap the major points:

|   |   |
|---|---|
|**GENERAL CONCEPTS**|   |
|**General Requirements**|- Approved by the IRS<br>- Tax benefits for employers and employees<br>- Must be permanent and have a vesting requirement<br>- Cannot discriminate in favor of the prohibited group|
|**TYPES OF PLANS**|   |
|**Individual Qualified Plans**|Traditional IRA requirements and features:<br><br>- Earned income<br>- Contributions:  <br>    - Pretax   <br>    - No limiting age  <br>    - Dollar limit - up to a maximum allowed amount  <br>    - Married couples - double the amount for singles<br>- Withdrawals must begin at age 59 ½ and not later than 73<br><br>Roth IRA:<br><br>- Earned income<br>- Contributions:  <br>    - After tax   <br>    - No limiting age   <br>    - Dollar limit - up to a maximum allowed amount<br>- Withdrawals do not have to begin at age 73|
|**Employer-sponsored Qualified Plans**|General characteristics:<br><br>- Contributions up to an IRS-specified amounts<br>- Both employer and employee can contribute<br><br>Types of plans:<br><br>- HR-10 (Keogh) - self-employed plans<br>- SEP - small employer/self-employed; employer funds an employee's IRA<br>- SIMPLE - small employer (no more than 100 employees); set up as IRA or as 401(k)<br>- 401(k) - any employer; cash or deferred arrangements; profit sharing<br>- 403(b) - tax-sheltered annuity (TSA) - nonprofit organizations|
